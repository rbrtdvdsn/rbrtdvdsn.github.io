title: Task-Relevant Maturity
---
When knowledge workers interview for new roles, management style is a common topic of conversation. Whether the interviewer is asking the candidate or vice-versa, we all want to know how we'll be managed and how our direct reports want to be managed.
This question has always seemed rooted in the idea that management style is fixed rather than fluid. Although that never sat right with me, I’d never interrogated my discomfort with that concept.
Just last year, when asked about my preferred management style in interviews for my current role with Copy.ai, I said "it depends" before immediately blurting out, "but I generally prefer a 'hands-off' approach." 
But here's the kicker, THAT'S NOT TRUE! I don't *always* prefer a "hands-off" approach!
I hoped to convey that I didn’t want or need a micro-manager.
But what I ended up conveying was, "Leave me alone. I work best on my own."
In reality, I want a manager who feels they're a partner in my development. I want to work for someone who:
* is willing to teach me what I don't know
* gives me opportunities at the edges of my competency
* will be honest and open with me about my performance 
* trusts me to do the work I’m best at without constant check-ins 
Enter my growth mentor [Andrew Capland][1] and the concept of this post, **task-relevant maturity**.
From Andy Grove's book *[High Output Management][2]*, task-relevant maturity is the concept that everyone has a different level of ability and motivation to accomplish any given task or project, and they will benefit most from a management style that matches their task-relevant maturity (TRM).
Someone with low TRM could be a new hire, trainee, or even a tenured employee who's stepping up to support a different team on a project outside their usual swim lane. People with low TRM will benefit most from a highly-structured approach that keeps them pointed in the right direction with logical next steps.
A person with medium TRM needs less detailed instructions but will still benefit from check-ins, emotional support, and encouragement throughout the project. Most management theory tackles this part of the spectrum, where the levels of skill and motivation can vary greatly on any given task.
And then there's high TRM, where someone is both highly skilled and motivated to accomplish the task or project and will benefit most from a manager who limits their involvement to milestone tracking and ensuring agreed-upon objectives and specs are met. 
While things have worked out well for me at Copy.ai, not everyone is so lucky. I've had six different managers and countless interviews thus far in my professional career. Not once had someone discussed management style as anything other than a one-to-one relationship with a one-style-fits-all approach.
I'm fortunate to now have the words and framework to move beyond that simplistic approach and ensure that my relationships with managers and direct reports are more nuanced and task-appropriate than they’ve been in the past.
